Formation

The Moon formed about 4.5 billion years ago, likely after a Mars-sized body collided with Earth. This impact ejected debris that eventually coalesced into the Moon.

Surface

The Moon's surface is covered in craters, dust, and rocky debris. Its landscape includes mountains, valleys, and flat plains called maria, formed by ancient volcanic activity.

Atmosphere

The Moon has a very thin atmosphere, called an exosphere, which cannot support life as we know it. This lack of atmosphere leads to extreme temperature variations.

Exploration

The Moon has been the target of human exploration since the 1960s. NASA's Apollo missions successfully landed astronauts on the lunar surface, with Apollo 11 being the first in 1969.

Gravity

The Moon's gravity is about one-sixth that of Earth's, allowing astronauts to jump much higher. This lower gravity affects the Moon's geological processes and surface features.

Phases

The Moon goes through eight distinct phases, from new moon to full moon and back again. These phases are caused by the changing angles of sunlight as the Moon orbits Earth.

Tidal Effects

The Moon's gravitational pull affects Earth's tides, creating high and low tides. This interaction plays a crucial role in the Earth's ecosystems and climate.

Future Missions

Upcoming missions aim to explore the Moon further, including NASA's Artemis program, which plans to return humans to the Moon and establish a sustainable presence by the end of the decade.
