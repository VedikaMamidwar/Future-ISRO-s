Explore the Black Hole

What is a Black Hole?

A black hole is a region in space where the gravitational pull is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape. It is formed when a massive star collapses under its own gravity at the end of its life cycle.

How Big Are They?

Black holes vary in size. The smallest black holes, known as stellar black holes, are about 10 times the mass of the sun, while supermassive black holes can be millions to billions of times more massive.

Event Horizon

The event horizon is the boundary around a black hole beyond which nothing can escape. It is the "point of no return" for any object falling into the black hole.

Singularity

At the center of a black hole lies a point called the singularity, where density and gravity become infinite, and the laws of physics break down.

Discovery of Black Holes

The concept of black holes dates back to the 18th century when John Michell and Pierre-Simon Laplace proposed the idea of "dark stars." However, the term "black hole" was coined by physicist John Archibald Wheeler in 1967. In 1971, astronomers discovered the first black hole, Cygnus X-1, which provided the first direct observational evidence of these mysterious objects in space.
